2.2 Frequency Distribution 
A frequency distribution is a table or graphical representation that displays the frequency of various outcomes or values in a dataset. It organizes data into categories or intervals and shows how many times each category occurs. Each entry in the frequency distribution table represents the count or frequency occurrence for a particular category or interval. Frequency distribution are commonly used in statistics and data analysis to summarize and understand the distribution of data. The provide insights into the central tendency, variability and shape of a dataset, helping analysis to identify patterns and trends within data.  
 
2.3 Percentage Analysis  
 Percentage analysis, also known as proportion analysis or ratio analysis, involves expressing individual components of a dataset as a percentage of a whole or relative to each other. It is a method used to compare different parts of a dataset to understand their relative importance, contribution, or distribution. Percentage analysis is also used in various other fields such as market research, demographics, and scientific research to interpret data and make comparisons. It helps to highlight trends, identify outliers, and understand the relative significance within a dataset. For Example: In financial analysis, percentage analysis is commonly used to analyze financial statements. You might express each line item on an income Watermen or balance sheet as a percentage of total revenue, total assets, or another relevant metric. This allows for easier comparison over time or between different companies of varying sizes.

2.5 Pearson's Chi-Square Test: 
 Pearson's chi-squared test: Pearson's x test is a statistical test applied to sets of categorical data to evaluate how likely it is that any observed difference between the sets arose by chance. It is the most widely used of many chi-squared tests statistical procedures whose results are evaluated by reference to the chi-squared distribution. Its properties were first investigated by Karl Pearson in 1900. In contexts where it is important to improve a distinction between the test statistic and its distribution, names similar to Pearson x-squared test or statistic are used.
